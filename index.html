    How to read the numbers

    Three metrics run through this whole page; full definitions live on the Metrics Guide tab.

    The metrics in one breath

    • Last50 / Last25 = who wins, weighted to recent form (shrunk toward 50%). Last50 (50-rack half-life) is the steady read; Last25 (25-rack half-life) is a leading indicator that spikes for hot players.
    • Shark /100 = who wins against tough competition and never gets hustled by weak players: each rack is worth points scaled to the opponent's handicap (beat a HC 7 = +0.71, lose to a HC 2 = −0.76).
    • Shark pts is the running total of those rack values.
    • 5+% = your win rate against the league's top half (HC 5+) — the playoff-caliber opponents. Shark can be padded by beating weak players; 5+ counts only the strong ones.
    • The six-season All board is limited to qualified players (50+ racks); single seasons show everyone who played, or flip Show \u2192 Qualified to keep only the regulars.

    Reference · How the numbers work

    The metrics, explained.

    Four numbers run through this whole site: Power, Shark points, Shark /100, and 5+%. Here's exactly what each one measures, the formula, and a worked example using real players.

    First, the foundation: the handicap ladder

    Both Shark metrics are built on this.

    Every handicap has a difficulty = how often that handicap wins across the whole league. A HC 7 wins ~71% of their racks, so beating one is "hard"; a HC 2 wins ~24%, so beating one is "easy." These numbers are the currency the Shark metrics trade in.

    1 · Last50 & Last25 — "recent form, plain and simple"

    Your straight win rate over your most recent racks — no weighting, no fuss.

    What it measures. Last50 is your win percentage over your most recent 50 racks; Last25 is the same over your last 25. That's it — wins ÷ racks across the freshest stretch of your record. Last50 is the leaderboard's default sort.

    Formula

    LastN = wins ÷ racks over your most recent N racks — racks counted newest-first across recent seasons, with the boundary season prorated so the window lands on exactly N.

    Why two windows. Last25 has the shorter memory — it moves fast when a player runs hot or cold. Last50 is steadier. When Last25 sits above Last50 a player is trending up; below, they're cooling. On a single-season view both collapse to that season's win rate; the split opens up on the all-time board and the player-page trend charts.

    PlayerLast25Last50Read
    Jerome Pulliam72%61%heating up
    Gene Heinrich61%63%steady
    Jerome's last 25 racks (72%) run well ahead of his last 50 (61%) — a clear hot streak after his big round. Gene's two windows sit right on top of each other: consistent, no recent swing.

    2 · Shark points — "quality of competition"

    Rewards beating strong players; punishes losing to weak ones.

    What it measures. Power treats every win the same. Shark doesn't — it values each rack by the opponent's difficulty (from the ladder above). Beating the top tier is worth a lot; getting hustled by a low seed costs a lot.

    Scoring

    A win earns the opponent's difficulty; a loss costs one minus it. Beat a HC 7 → +0.71. Beat a HC 2 → +0.24. Lose to a HC 2 → −0.76. Lose to a HC 7 → −0.29. Sum over every rack.

    Gene's +44.5 is huge because he wins against the top of the field (that 22–20 vs HC 7 alone is worth +9.7). A player with the same overall record built on beating only weak opponents would score far lower — that's the whole point.

    3 · Shark /100 — "quality, per rack"

    The volume-neutral version of Shark points.

    What it measures. Total Shark points reward volume — play more, accumulate more. Shark /100 divides by racks so you can compare a 250-rack player to a 700-rack player fairly: it's the average value earned per 100 racks.

    Formula

    Shark /100 = Shark points ÷ racks × 100

    PlayerShark pointsRacksShark /100
    Gene Heinrich+44.5252+17.7
    Neal Plofker+40.0728+5.5
    Neal's total (+40.0) is nearly Gene's — because Neal plays almost 3× the racks. But per 100 racks, Gene (+17.7) is on a completely different level. Use total to ask "who's provided the most value overall"; use /100 to ask "who's the strongest, rack for rack."

    4 · 5+ — "performance vs the top half"

    How you do against the strong players — the caliber you meet in the playoffs.

    What it measures. Your straight win rate against HC 5 and above — the league's top half. HC 5 is exactly where leaguewide win rate crosses 50% (a HC 5 wins ~56% of their racks; a HC 4 only ~45%), and just about half of all racks in the league are played against this group. 5+ ignores the bottom half entirely and asks the playoff question: when the opponent is genuinely good, how often do you actually win?

    Formula

    5+% = wins vs HC 5+ ÷ (wins + losses vs HC 5+)

    Why it sits next to Shark. Shark rewards quality across all opponents and punishes getting upset by weak ones — so a tidy Shark can come from simply never losing to the bottom half. 5+ can't be padded that way: only the strong half counts. Together they separate "avoids upsets" from "beats the best."

    PlayerShark /100Record vs HC 5+5+%
    Gene Heinrich+17.578–5857%
    Brett Calkins+4.923–4136%
    Both carry a positive Shark, but they earn it differently. Gene genuinely beats the top half more than half the time (57%). Brett's Shark is buoyed by rarely getting upset by weaker players — against the strong half he wins just 36%. Come playoff time, when the weak half disappears, that gap is the whole story.

    "Playing like" tiers

    The banded right axis on each player's season Shark chart.

    A player's Shark /100 tells you what handicap they're playing like that season. Each handicap owns a distinct Shark /100 band — the boundaries are the midpoints between neighboring handicaps' typical (median) marks across 174 qualified players. Your season lands in exactly one band; that's the grade you played like, regardless of your official handicap.
