    How to read the numbers

    Three metrics run through this whole page; full definitions live on the Metrics Guide tab.

    The metrics in one breath

    • Power50 / Power25 = who wins, weighted to recent form (shrunk toward 50%). Power50 (50-rack half-life) is the steady read; Power25 (25-rack half-life) is a leading indicator that spikes for hot players.
    • Shark /100 = who wins against tough competition and never gets hustled by weak players: each rack is worth points scaled to the opponent's handicap (beat a HC 7 = +0.71, lose to a HC 2 = −0.76).
    • Shark pts is the running total of those rack values.
    • The six-season All board is limited to qualified players (50+ racks); a single-season view shows everyone who played it.

    Reference · How the numbers work

    The metrics, explained.

    Three numbers run through this whole site: Power, Shark points, and Shark /100. Here's exactly what each one measures, the formula, and a worked example using real players.

    First, the foundation: the handicap ladder

    Both Shark metrics are built on this.

    Every handicap has a difficulty = how often that handicap wins across the whole league. A HC 7 wins ~71% of their racks, so beating one is "hard"; a HC 2 wins ~24%, so beating one is "easy." These numbers are the currency the Shark metrics trade in.

    1 · Power50 & Power25 — "who's winning, right now"

    A recency-weighted, small-sample-proof win rate, in two flavours.

    What it measures. Straight win rate, but with two adjustments so it reflects current form and doesn't get fooled by a hot streak. It's the default sort on the leaderboard.

    How it's built.

    1. Recency weight. Every rack is discounted by how long ago it was played, on a 50-rack half-life — a rack ~50 racks back counts half as much, ~100 back a quarter. Effectively the last season or two carry the load.
    2. Shrinkage. Add 12 pseudo-racks of 50/50 record so a 6–0 start can't rocket a thin record to the top; a big sample barely feels it.

    Two flavours, named by half-life. Power50 (50-rack half-life, cutoff = last 160 games) is the steady read and the leaderboard default. Power25 (25-rack half-life, cutoff = last 80 games) is a leading indicator: it weights the newest racks much harder, so it spikes when a player gets hot and sags when they go cold — same formula, shorter memory. Each player page charts both over time.

    Formula

    PowerH = (Σ w·wins + 6) / (Σ w·racks + 12), where w = 0.5^(racks-ago / H) and H is the half-life (25 or 50). Games past the cutoff (80 for Power25, 160 for Power50) carry <10% weight and are dropped.

    Worked example — Neal's Power50. His five most recent seasons, each weighted by recency (Power25 uses the same math with a faster 25-rack half-life):

    2 · Shark points — "quality of competition"

    Rewards beating strong players; punishes losing to weak ones.

    What it measures. Power treats every win the same. Shark doesn't — it values each rack by the opponent's difficulty (from the ladder above). Beating the top tier is worth a lot; getting hustled by a low seed costs a lot.

    Scoring

    A win earns the opponent's difficulty; a loss costs one minus it. Beat a HC 7 → +0.71. Beat a HC 2 → +0.24. Lose to a HC 2 → −0.76. Lose to a HC 7 → −0.29. Sum over every rack.

    Gene's +44.5 is huge because he wins against the top of the field (that 22–20 vs HC 7 alone is worth +9.7). A player with the same overall record built on beating only weak opponents would score far lower — that's the whole point.

    3 · Shark /100 — "quality, per rack"

    The volume-neutral version of Shark points.

    What it measures. Total Shark points reward volume — play more, accumulate more. Shark /100 divides by racks so you can compare a 250-rack player to a 700-rack player fairly: it's the average value earned per 100 racks.

    Formula

    Shark /100 = Shark points ÷ racks × 100

    PlayerShark pointsRacksShark /100
    Gene Heinrich+44.5252+17.7
    Neal Plofker+40.0728+5.5
    Neal's total (+40.0) is nearly Gene's — because Neal plays almost 3× the racks. But per 100 racks, Gene (+17.7) is on a completely different level. Use total to ask "who's provided the most value overall"; use /100 to ask "who's the strongest, rack for rack."

    "Playing like" tiers

    The banded right axis on each player's season Shark chart.

    A player's Shark /100 tells you what handicap they're playing like that season. Each handicap owns a distinct Shark /100 band — the boundaries are the midpoints between neighboring handicaps' typical (median) marks across 174 qualified players. Your season lands in exactly one band; that's the grade you played like, regardless of your official handicap.
